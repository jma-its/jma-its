Energy, Work, Power, Efficiency and Heat Engines

How heat and fuel become useful motion.

Today we move from simple machines to engines. Simple machines help us use force. Engines give machines power. To understand engines, we need four ideas: work, energy, power, and efficiency.

Big question

How can heat and fuel become motion?

Main chain

fuel → heat → pressure → piston → rotation

Engineering idea

Machines do not create energy. They transform energy.

1. Work

In engineering, work has a special meaning. Work means force with movement.

If you push a wall, you use force. But the wall does not move. So there is no useful mechanical work.

If you push a hospital bed and the bed moves, you do mechanical work.

W = F × s

W = work, joule (J)
F = force, newton (N)
s = distance, meter (m)

Example

A nurse pushes a hospital bed.

Force = 60 N
Distance = 3 m

W = F × s = 60 × 3 = 180 J

Try it

Force = 40 N
Distance = 2 m
Work = ?

Show answer

W = F × s = 40 × 2 = 80 J

2. Energy

Energy means ability to do work.

A person gets energy from food. An engine gets energy from fuel. A battery stores electrical energy. A moving part has mechanical energy.

Machines do not create energy from nothing. A machine changes energy from one form into another form.

Energy transformation

chemical energy → heat energy → mechanical motion

For heat engines, the most important idea is this: fuel has chemical energy. Burning fuel makes heat. Heat makes gas pressure. Pressure can move a piston.

3. Power

Power means how fast work is done.

Two machines can do the same work. One machine does it slowly. Another machine does it quickly. The faster machine has more power.

P = W / t

P = power, watt (W)
W = work, joule (J)
t = time, second (s)

Important: Work uses the symbol W. Watt also uses the letter W as a unit. This is confusing, but it is normal in physics.

Example

Work = 180 J
Time = 6 s

P = W / t = 180 / 6 = 30 W

Try it

Work = 200 J
Time = 10 s
Power = ?

Show answer

P = W / t = 200 / 10 = 20 W

4. Efficiency

No real machine is perfect.

We put energy into a machine. Some energy becomes useful output. Some energy is lost as heat, sound, vibration, and friction.

η = useful output / input × 100%

η is efficiency. Efficiency is usually written in percent.

High efficiency means small losses. Low efficiency means big losses.

Example

Input energy = 100 J
Useful output = 80 J

η = 80 / 100 × 100% = 80%

20 J is lost.

Try it

Input energy = 200 J
Useful output = 150 J
Efficiency = ?

Show answer

η = 150 / 200 × 100% = 75%

5. Percent to decimal

This is very important for efficiency calculations.

When we multiply efficiencies, we must first change percent into decimal form.

90% = 0.90

Divide by 100: 90 ÷ 100 = 0.90

Percent → decimal

  • 80% = 0.80
  • 75% = 0.75
  • 60% = 0.60
  • 50.4% = 0.504

Decimal → percent

  • 0.72 = 72%
  • 0.50 = 50%
  • 0.504 = 50.4%

Try it

Change to decimal:
85% = ?
40% = ?

Show answer

85% = 0.85
40% = 0.40

6. Multi-stage efficiency

A real machine often has many parts. Energy goes step by step: motor, belt, gearbox, pump.

Each part loses some energy. Small losses can become one big loss.

ηtotal = η₁ × η₂ × η₃

Use decimal numbers, not percent numbers.

Example

Belt efficiency = 90% = 0.90
Gearbox efficiency = 80% = 0.80
Pump efficiency = 70% = 0.70

ηtotal = 0.90 × 0.80 × 0.70 = 0.504 = 50.4%

Almost half of the input energy is lost.

Try it

Stage 1 efficiency = 90%
Stage 2 efficiency = 80%
Total efficiency = ?

Show answer

90% = 0.90
80% = 0.80
0.90 × 0.80 = 0.72 = 72%

7. Useful output power

Efficiency can also help us calculate useful output power.

Useful output power = input power × efficiency

Again, change percent to decimal first.

Example

Input power = 500 W
Total efficiency = 60% = 0.60

Useful output power = 500 × 0.60 = 300 W

Try it

Input power = 100 W
Total efficiency = 72%
Useful output power = ?

Show answer

72% = 0.72
100 × 0.72 = 72 W

8. Heat and temperature

Temperature and heat are not the same.

Temperature

How hot something is.

Heat

Energy moving from a hot object to a cold object.

A hot engine gives heat to the air. Hot steam gives heat to metal parts. Heat flow is important in all heat engines.

9. Gas particles and temperature

Gas is made of tiny moving particles.

Cold gas particles move slowly. Hot gas particles move faster. Fast particles hit the walls harder. This creates more pressure.

higher temperature → faster particles → more pressure

10. Pressure

Pressure means force on area. Pressure is very important in engines because pressure can push a piston.

P = F / A

P = pressure, pascal (Pa)
F = force, newton (N)
A = area, square meter (m²)

F = P × A

For engines, this form is very useful: pressure makes force.

Example

Pressure = 100,000 Pa
Piston area = 0.01 m²

F = P × A = 100,000 × 0.01 = 1,000 N

Try it

Pressure = 200,000 Pa
Piston area = 0.02 m²
Force = ?

Show answer

F = P × A = 200,000 × 0.02 = 4,000 N

11. Gas expansion

When gas becomes hot, it expands. Expand means it tries to take more space.

Inside a cylinder, gas cannot go everywhere. So the hot gas pushes the piston.

hot gas expands → pressure pushes piston

This is the basic idea of many engines.

12. Steam engine principle

A steam engine uses heat. Fire heats water. Water becomes steam. Steam has pressure. Steam pressure pushes a piston.

fuel → fire → water → steam → pressure → piston

The steam engine changes heat energy into mechanical motion.

13. From piston to rotation

A piston moves in a straight line. But many machines need rotation.

A connecting rod and crankshaft change straight motion into rotation.

linear motion → crankshaft → rotation

This idea is used in steam engines and combustion engines.

14. Steam engine energy chain

Follow the energy step by step:

  1. Fuel has chemical energy.
  2. Burning fuel gives heat.
  3. Heat makes steam.
  4. Steam pressure pushes the piston.
  5. Piston motion becomes rotation.

15. Internal combustion engine

In a steam engine, burning is outside the cylinder. In an internal combustion engine, fuel burns inside the cylinder.

Fuel and air enter the cylinder. Burning makes hot gas. Hot gas pressure pushes the piston. The piston turns the crankshaft.

fuel + air → burn → hot gas → pressure → piston → rotation

16. Four strokes

A common gasoline engine uses four strokes. A stroke means one movement of the piston.

  1. Intake: fuel and air go in.
  2. Compression: the piston squeezes them.
  3. Power: spark, burning, hot gas pushes the piston.
  4. Exhaust: dirty gas goes out.

This cycle repeats many times per second.

17. Steam engine vs combustion engine

Steam engine

Burning is outside the cylinder.

Water becomes steam.

Steam pushes the piston.

Combustion engine

Burning is inside the cylinder.

Fuel burns in the cylinder.

Hot gas pushes the piston.

Both engines use pressure, piston motion, and crankshaft rotation.

18. Gasoline and diesel

Both gasoline and diesel engines are combustion engines, but burning starts differently.

Gasoline engine

A spark starts burning.

Diesel engine

High compression starts burning.

Both change chemical energy in fuel into mechanical motion.

19. Where heat engines are used

Heat engines are used in cars, ambulances, ships, trains, construction machines, and backup generators.

Hospitals need electricity all the time. If main power stops, a backup generator can help. A fuel engine turns a generator, and the generator makes electricity.

20. Troubleshooting idea

If an engine does not work, do not panic. Ask simple questions first.

  1. Is there fuel?
  2. Is there air?
  3. Is there ignition?
  4. Is there compression?
  5. Can the moving parts move?
  6. Is something blocked or broken?

This is the same engineering habit as before: look first, think first, test step by step.

22. Summary

  • Work = force × distance.
  • Power = work / time.
  • Efficiency shows useful output.
  • For multi-stage machines, multiply efficiencies in decimal form.
  • Heat can make gas pressure.
  • Pressure can push a piston.
  • A crankshaft changes straight motion into rotation.
  • Steam engines use steam pressure.
  • Combustion engines use hot gas pressure.

Engineering means: take energy, control it, and make useful work.
