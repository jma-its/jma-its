Part 1 — Why Materials Matter

This is a self-study lesson. Read slowly. Think before opening the answers.

The main idea is simple: material → property → use.

Opening

Introduction — Why Materials Matter

Technology is not only about machines or computers.

Everything around us is made of materials. A hospital bed, a syringe, gloves, a chair, a tube, a mask, and a wheelchair all use different materials.

Materials matter because they decide how objects behave. If we choose the wrong material, even a simple object can become useless or dangerous.

In hospitals this is very important because doctors and patients depend on equipment.

Material→Property→Use

Think and answer

Look around you. Name one object and one material in it.

Show answer

Example: chair → plastic and metal; mask → fabric or filter material; phone → glass, metal, plastic.

Core idea

Materials Decide Behavior

Different materials have different properties.

Metal can be strong. Rubber is flexible. Plastic is light. Glass is hard but can break.

A property is something a material can do or cannot do. Strong, flexible, light, hard, soft, brittle, clean, safe, waterproof — these are examples of properties.

When engineers choose a material, they do not choose randomly. They ask: what job must this object do?

Think and answer

Which material is flexible: metal, rubber, plastic, or glass?

Show answer

Rubber is usually flexible. Some plastics can also be flexible, but rubber is the clearest answer here.

Hospital safety

Wrong Material = Wrong Function

If the material is wrong, the object may fail.

If we choose the wrong material, the object will not work well. A bed can break. Gloves can tear. A tube can crack.

In a hospital, this can be dangerous. A failed bed can hurt a patient. Torn gloves can reduce protection. A cracked tube can leak.

That is why material choice is not only an engineering topic. It is also a safety topic.

Think and answer

Why is a cracked tube dangerous in a hospital?

Show answer

It can leak liquid, medicine, air, or oxygen. It may stop the system from working safely.

Engineering thinking

What Engineers Ask

Engineers connect material, property, and use.

Engineers must ask simple but important questions.

What material is best? Why is it used here? What happens if we change it?

These questions help us avoid silly or dangerous designs.

What material?→What property?→What use?

Think and answer

Why should engineers ask “What happens if we change it?”

Show answer

Because changing material can change strength, flexibility, safety, comfort, cleaning, cost, and durability.

Key tool

Material → Property → Use

This is the thinking tool for the lesson.

Material means what the object is made from.

Property means how the material behaves.

Use means the job of the object.

A good design connects all three. A bad design breaks the chain.

Material→Property→Use

Think and answer

For surgical gloves, what property is important?

Show answer

They must be flexible, clean, protective, and not easy to tear.

Review

Bad Material Choices: Explanations

The slide gives the same idea in compact form.

Each bad example fails because the material property does not match the use.

For example, wood is not a good syringe material because it is difficult to clean and may break. Glass is not good for gloves because it is brittle. Sponge is not good for wheelchair wheels because it is too soft.

The lesson is simple: material choice must match the job.

Think and answer

What is the common mistake in all these examples?

Show answer

The material property does not match the object’s use.
