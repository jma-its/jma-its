Slide 3

1. Electric charge

Electricity starts with charge. There are two types of electric charge: positive charge and negative charge.

Same charges repel. This means they push away from each other. Opposite charges attract. This means they pull toward each other.

same charges repel
opposite charges attract

This is the first basic rule. We will use it later when we talk about electric fields, voltage, and current.

Slide 4

2. Coulomb's law

Coulomb's law describes the force between two electric charges.

F = k q₁ q₂ / r²

F = electric force
q₁, q₂ = charges
r = distance between charges
k = constant

Meaning

  • Bigger charges give bigger force.
  • Bigger distance gives much smaller force.
  • The force changes with the square of distance.

Today we do not need difficult Coulomb calculations. The important idea is simple: electric charges can create force at a distance.

Slide 5

3. Electric field

A charge creates an electric field around it. A field is an invisible area of influence. You cannot see the field directly, but you can see its effect.

The electric field can push or pull another charge. Field lines show the direction of force on a positive test charge.

E = F / q

E = electric field
F = force
q = test charge

Simple meaning: charge creates electric field, and electric field creates force on other charges.

Slides 6–7

4. Voltage, current and resistance

Now we move from electrostatics to electric circuits. In electrostatics, charges can be at rest. In a circuit, charges move. Moving charge is current.

Voltage

Voltage is electric push. A battery or power supply gives voltage.

Current

Current is flow of electric charge through a wire.

Resistance

Resistance opposes current. High resistance makes current smaller.

Memory sentence: Voltage pushes. Current flows. Resistance opposes.

Slide 7

5. Basic electrical formulas

These are the main calculation formulas for this lecture.

I = Q / t

Current = charge / time.

V = I R

Ohm's law. Voltage = current × resistance.

P = V I

Electrical power = voltage × current.

Example

Voltage = 12 V
Current = 2 A

P = V × I = 12 × 2 = 24 W

Try it

Current = 3 A
Resistance = 4 Ω
Voltage = ?

Show answer

V = I × R = 3 × 4 = 12 V

Slide 8

6. Magnets and magnetic field

A magnet has two poles: north pole and south pole. Like magnetic poles repel. Opposite magnetic poles attract.

A magnet creates a magnetic field around it. Magnetic field lines show the direction of magnetic field. Outside a magnet, the field lines go from N to S.

Electric motors need magnetic fields. Without a magnetic field, we cannot explain why a motor turns.

Slide 9

7. Current creates magnetic field

This is one of the most important ideas in the whole lecture.

When electric current flows in a wire, a magnetic field appears around the wire. The magnetic field lines make circles around the wire.

current → magnetic field

If the current becomes stronger, the magnetic field becomes stronger. If we change the direction of current, the direction of the magnetic field also changes.

Slide 10

8. Two parallel wires

Two wires with current can push or pull each other. This is not electrostatic force. It is magnetic force caused by current.

same current direction → attract
opposite current directions → repel

This idea is useful because a motor has many conductors and coils. Current in conductors creates magnetic fields, and these magnetic fields create forces.

Slide 11

9. Force on a moving charge

A moving charge in a magnetic field feels force. The direction of the force is perpendicular to the velocity and also perpendicular to the magnetic field.

F = q v B sinθ

q = charge
v = velocity
B = magnetic field
θ = angle between velocity and magnetic field

Meaning: if the charge moves parallel to the magnetic field, the force is zero. If it moves perpendicular to the field, the force is maximum.

Slide 12

10. Force on a current-carrying conductor

A wire with current is full of moving charges. So a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field also feels force.

F = B I l sinθ

B = magnetic field
I = current
l = length of wire in the magnetic field
θ = angle between current direction and magnetic field

This is the main formula behind a simple electric motor.

Concept check

When is the force maximum?

Show answer

The force is maximum when the current direction is perpendicular to the magnetic field. Then sinθ = 1.

Slides 14–15

11. Current loop in a magnetic field

Now we can build the idea of a motor step by step.

A rectangular loop is placed between N and S magnetic poles. Current flows around the loop. The magnetic field creates forces on the two vertical sides of the loop.

One side feels force upward. The other side feels force downward. These opposite forces make a turning effect. This turning effect is called torque.

opposite forces → torque → rotation
Slide 15

12. Torque on a loop

Torque means turning force. A current loop in a magnetic field feels torque.

τ = N B I A sinθ

τ = torque
N = number of turns
B = magnetic field
I = current
A = area of loop
θ = angle

Meaning: more turns, stronger magnetic field, more current, and larger loop area give more torque.

Slide 16

13. Why a simple loop stops

A simple current loop is not enough for a real motor. At one position, the torque becomes zero. This is called a dead point.

If nothing changes, the loop can stop or the torque direction can become wrong. So we need a switching system.

simple loop + no switching → not continuous rotation
Slides 17–18

14. Split-ring commutator and brushes

The split-ring commutator solves the problem. It reverses the current every half turn.

Brushes touch the commutator and connect the moving rotor to the external circuit. The brushes stay still. The commutator rotates with the coil.

commutator = automatic current switch

Because the current reverses at the right time, the torque keeps helping the same rotation direction.

Slides 19–20

15. From one loop to many coils

One loop is useful for explanation, but real motors are not built from only one loop.

Real motors use several coils. More coils give smoother rotation, more stable torque, and better performance.

Three coils arranged 120° apart are an important step toward real rotating machines and also toward the idea of a rotating magnetic field.

Slide 21

16. DC motor structure

Now we can understand a DC motor as a real machine, not as a black box.

  • Stator: fixed part that provides magnetic field.
  • Rotor: rotating part with coils.
  • Commutator: switching contact that reverses current.
  • Brushes: electrical contacts to the rotating commutator.
  • Shaft: gives useful mechanical rotation outside the motor.
Slide 22

17. Motor energy chain

The motor changes electrical energy into mechanical motion.

electricity → magnetic field → force → torque → rotation

This chain is the main idea of an electric motor. In hospital and IT equipment, motors use this principle to move fans, pumps, beds, doors, and many other parts.

Slides 23–25

18. Faraday's idea

Now we ask the opposite question.

A motor changes electricity into motion. Can motion create electricity?

Faraday showed that a changing magnetic field can create voltage. More exactly: changing magnetic flux induces voltage.

changing magnetic flux → induced voltage
Slide 24

19. Magnetic flux

Magnetic flux means how much magnetic field passes through a surface or loop.

Φ = B A cosθ

Φ = magnetic flux
B = magnetic field
A = area
θ = angle between magnetic field and area vector

When a loop rotates, the angle changes. When the angle changes, the flux changes. This is why a rotating coil can generate voltage.

Slide 25

20. Faraday's law

Faraday's law connects changing flux with induced voltage.

ε = − dΦ / dt

ε = induced voltage
Φ = magnetic flux
dΦ/dt = how fast the flux changes

Simple meaning: if magnetic flux changes quickly, induced voltage is larger. If magnetic flux does not change, there is no induced voltage.

The minus sign shows the direction rule. Today, we only need the main idea: changing flux makes voltage.

Slides 26–27

21. Generator principle

A generator uses Faraday's law. A coil rotates in a magnetic field. The magnetic flux through the coil changes. Voltage appears at the output.

motion → changing flux → induced voltage → electricity

A generator changes mechanical motion into electrical energy.

Slides 28–30

22. Tesla, AC and rotating magnetic field

Nikola Tesla is important here not as a myth, but as an engineer and inventor connected with AC power and rotating magnetic fields.

Three-phase AC currents can feed three coils placed around a stator. The currents are shifted in time. The result is a magnetic field that rotates smoothly.

three-phase AC → rotating magnetic field → AC motor

This idea made many practical AC motors possible. AC motors are used in industry, buildings, pumps, fans, and many power systems.

Slide 31

23. Applications in hospitals and IT

This lecture is not only theory. Motors and generators are everywhere in real medical and IT systems.

  • Infusion pumps use motors to move liquid carefully.
  • Hospital beds use motors or actuators to change position.
  • Printers use motors to move paper and rollers.
  • Cooling fans use motors to remove heat from electronics.
  • Automatic doors use motors for safe movement.
  • Backup power systems use generators to keep important equipment running.
Practical thinking

24. Troubleshooting idea

If a motor or electric device does not work, do not panic. Think step by step.

  1. Is there power?
  2. Is the cable connected?
  3. Is the switch on?
  4. Is there a blown fuse or protection stop?
  5. Is the motor blocked?
  6. Is there overheating?
  7. Is there strange sound or vibration?
  8. If unsafe, stop and call a technician.

This is useful for future work with hospital and IT equipment.

Slide 34

26. Summary

  • Charge creates electric field.
  • Voltage pushes. Current flows. Resistance opposes current.
  • Current creates magnetic field.
  • A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field feels force.
  • A current loop feels torque.
  • A commutator keeps a DC motor turning in the same direction.
  • Many coils make smoother rotation.
  • Changing magnetic flux induces voltage.
  • Motor: electricity → motion.
  • Generator: motion → electricity.
  • Tesla's rotating magnetic field idea is important for AC motors.

The big engineering idea is simple: electricity and magnetism can create controlled motion, and controlled motion can create electricity.
