Part 1 — Why Materials Behave Differently

The main question is simple: why does one material return to shape, while another stays bent or breaks?

Opening

Properties of Materials

Elastic and plastic deformation, alloys, heat treatment, and mechanical properties.

In the previous lesson, we learned that material choice matters. Now we go deeper.

We study why materials behave differently inside: atomic order, slipping, alloys, and heat treatment.

This matters for medical tools and hospital equipment because materials must be safe, reliable, and appropriate for their job.

Think and answer

What is one medical object where material strength matters?

Show answer

Examples: surgical tool, hospital bed, wheelchair frame, needle, screw, clamp.

Main question

Why Does One Wire Return and Another Stay Bent?

Same force can give different results.

Imagine two wires. You apply the same force to both. One returns to its original shape. The other stays bent.

This happens because materials have different internal structure and mechanical properties.

Today we learn the difference between elastic deformation and plastic deformation.

Force→Material response→Elastic or Plastic

Think and answer

If a wire returns to its original shape, what kind of deformation is it?

Show answer

Elastic deformation.

Part 2 — Inside Metals: Atoms and Crystal Lattice

To understand deformation, we must look inside the metal.

Atomic structure

Atomic Crystal Structure of Metals

Metals have a regular crystal lattice.

A metal is made of atoms arranged in an orderly pattern. This pattern is called a crystal lattice.

You can imagine atoms like people standing in straight rows. The order gives the metal important properties.

Different structures exist, such as FCC or BCC. The important idea is: inside structure affects outside behavior.

Think and answer

Why do we care about atoms when talking about hospital tools?

Show answer

Because atom arrangement affects strength, hardness, bending, breaking, and safety.

Extra concept

Metallic Bonding: Electron Sea

This idea helps explain why metals can stretch a little and conduct electricity.

In a metal, positive metal ions are arranged in the lattice. Around them there are many free electrons.

These electrons can move through the metal and help hold the ions together. This is often called an electron sea.

This section is text-only here because the current slide deck does not include a separate bonding picture.

Think and answer

Why can metals conduct electricity well?

Show answer

Because they have free electrons that can move through the metal.

Part 3 — Elastic and Plastic Deformation

Small force usually causes elastic deformation. Large force can cause plastic deformation.

Elastic deformation

Elastic Deformation

Atoms move a little, then return.

Elastic deformation happens when the force is not too large.

Atoms move slightly from their normal positions. They stretch or compress, but do not permanently change places.

When the force is removed, the atoms return. The material returns to its original shape.

Think and answer

What happens after the force is removed in elastic deformation?

Show answer

The material returns to its original shape.

Plastic deformation

Plastic Deformation

Atoms slide permanently along slip planes.

Plastic deformation happens when the force is large enough.

Atoms do not only stretch. Groups of atoms slide past each other along slip planes.

After the force is removed, the atoms do not return. The material keeps the new shape.

Think and answer

If a spoon stays bent after force is removed, what happened?

Show answer

Plastic deformation.

Comparison

Elastic vs Plastic Deformation

The key difference is return or permanent change.

Elastic deformation: small force, atoms stretch and return, shape comes back.

Plastic deformation: larger force, atoms slide permanently, shape stays changed.

Some medical parts must return to shape. Other parts can be permanently shaped during manufacturing.

Elastic

Returns to original shape after force is removed.

Plastic

Keeps the new shape after force is removed.

Think and answer

Which is better for a spring?

Show answer

Elastic behavior, because a spring must return to its original shape.

Part 4 — Alloys and Heat Treatment

Pure metals are often not enough. Engineers change materials to get better properties.

Alloys

Alloys

An alloy is a mixture of two or more metals or elements.

Pure metals are often too soft or too weak for real tools.

An alloy is designed to improve properties. Bronze is copper plus tin. Steel is iron plus carbon.

In substitution solid solution, some atoms replace other atoms. In interstitial solid solution, small atoms fit between larger atoms.

Think and answer

What is steel made from in simple terms?

Show answer

Iron plus carbon.

Why alloys are stronger

Why Are Alloys Stronger?

Different atoms disturb easy sliding.

In pure metal, atoms are often the same size and layers may slide more easily.

In an alloy, different sized atoms disturb the perfect lattice. This makes slipping more difficult.

If atoms cannot slide easily, the material can become stronger and harder.

Different atoms→Disturbed lattice→Harder sliding→Stronger material

Think and answer

Why can adding carbon make iron stronger?

Show answer

Carbon atoms disturb the iron lattice and make atomic sliding more difficult.

Heat treatment

Heat Treatment and Quenching

Controlled heating and cooling can change properties.

Heat treatment means controlled heating and cooling of metal.

Quenching means heating metal very hot, then cooling it very quickly in water or oil.

After quenching, metal can become harder and stronger. But it can also become more brittle.

Think and answer

What is a possible disadvantage of quenching?

Show answer

The metal may become more brittle.

Visual step

Quenching Visual

Hot metal is cooled very quickly.

The picture shows how slow cooling and rapid cooling can produce different internal structures.

Fast cooling changes the internal structure of the metal.

Engineers use this carefully because improving one property can hurt another.

Think and answer

Why must heat treatment be controlled?

Show answer

Bad control can make the part too brittle or unsuitable.

Case study

Japanese Katana: Materials Science Example

The katana combines a hard edge and tougher core.

The edge is high-carbon and quenched, so it becomes very hard and sharp.

The core is softer and more flexible, so the sword can absorb energy and resist breaking.

This is the golden mean idea: not maximum hardness only, but a balance of properties.

Think and answer

Why is a hard edge useful?

Show answer

It stays sharp and cuts well.

Part 5 — Mechanical Properties

These words help us choose materials for medical tools and hospital equipment.

Properties list

Mechanical Properties of Materials

Engineers need precise words for material behavior.

Hardness: resistance to scratching or indentation.

Strength: ability to withstand load without breaking.

Elasticity: ability to return to original shape. Plasticity: ability to change shape permanently.

Brittleness: breaks without much plastic deformation. Toughness: ability to absorb energy before breaking.

Think and answer

Which property means ability to return to original shape?

Show answer

Elasticity.

Hardness

Hardness

Hardness means resistance to scratching or denting.

Hardness tells us how difficult it is to scratch or dent a material.

Rockwell, Brinell, and Vickers tests press a hard ball or diamond tip into the surface.

Medical cutting tools need high hardness so they stay sharp.

Think and answer

Why do surgical cutting tools need hardness?

Show answer

They must stay sharp and cut cleanly.

Strength and toughness

Strength and Toughness

Strength and toughness are not the same.

Strength is how much load a material can carry without breaking.

Toughness is how much energy a material can absorb before it breaks.

A good medical tool often needs both: it must resist force and not break suddenly.

Think and answer

Which property is about absorbing energy before breaking?

Show answer

Toughness.

Important task

Mild Steel vs White Cast Iron

Harder does not always mean better in tension.

Material 1: low-carbon mild steel. It is relatively soft, bends easily, and has good ductility.

Material 2: white cast iron. It is very hard, but brittle and almost does not bend.

Which material better resists breaking when we pull it? Many students choose cast iron because it is hard. That is a common mistake.

Low-carbon mild steel usually resists tensile breaking better because it can stretch and absorb energy. White cast iron can crack suddenly.

Think and answer

Which is better under tensile pulling: low-carbon mild steel or white cast iron?

Show answer

Low-carbon mild steel. It is softer, but it has ductility and toughness. White cast iron is hard but brittle.

Part 6 — Summary and Final Activity

Connect atomic structure, deformation, alloys, heat treatment, and mechanical properties.

Summary

What You Should Remember

Materials behave differently because their internal structure and processing are different.

Elastic deformation returns to shape. Plastic deformation stays changed.

Alloys can be stronger because different atoms disturb easy sliding.

Heat treatment can increase hardness and strength, but may increase brittleness.

Good engineering is balance: enough hardness, strength, ductility, and toughness for the job.

Structure→Processing→Properties→Use
